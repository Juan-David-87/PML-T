CRISP-ML(Q) Phase

Data Understanding

This phase focuses on understanding the structure, quality, completeness, and analytical potential of the selected dataset. The analysis includes exploratory data analysis, variable inspection, missing value evaluation, statistical summaries, data quality assessment, and correlation analysis.

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

Dataset Exploration

An exploratory data analysis (EDA) was conducted to understand the structure, organization, quality, and analytical characteristics of the dataset before future preprocessing and predictive modeling stages. This process allowed a preliminary understanding of how historical digital government indicators are distributed across Colombian territorial entities.

The exploratory process focused on dataset dimensions, variable types, historical observations, territorial classifications, and digital government indicators associated with municipalities, departments, and institutional entities. The dataset structure was reviewed to better understand how information is organized and how it may support future machine learning implementation.

Variable Analysis

Feature Inspection

Numerical variables provide measurable information related to performance indicators, territorial identifiers, historical validity, and institutional measurements. These variables may support descriptive statistics, predictive modeling, and future feature engineering activities.

Categorical variables provide contextual information that allows territorial grouping, institutional classification, and municipality-level comparisons. Their interpretation is important to understand how territorial and administrative differences may influence digital development conditions across Colombia.

Understanding variable behavior is essential for future preprocessing decisions, variable selection, encoding strategies, and the construction of meaningful analytical features for machine learning purposes.

Missing Value Analysis

Data Completeness

Missing value analysis was conducted to identify incomplete observations and evaluate the completeness of historical, institutional, and territorial information contained in the dataset. Understanding missing information is important to determine whether incomplete observations may negatively affect future analytical and predictive stages.

Missing observations may reflect unavailable reporting, inconsistencies in data collection processes, institutional limitations, or historical variations in governmental records. In some cases, missing information may represent unavailable administrative reporting rather than measurement errors.

The inspection process focused on detecting empty values, incomplete records, and variables with inconsistent levels of completeness. This evaluation helps define future strategies for data treatment, including imputation methods, variable filtering, or standardization procedures.

Evaluating missing information contributes to improving transparency, analytical consistency, and reliability during preprocessing and future machine learning implementation.

Data Quality Evaluation

Reliability Assessment

Data quality evaluation focused on understanding the reliability, consistency, and usability of the selected dataset for exploratory analysis and future predictive modeling activities. Since the information comes from historical public records, it was necessary to inspect possible inconsistencies that could affect interpretation.

The evaluation process included duplicated observations, formatting inconsistencies, territorial naming differences, incomplete records, and possible structural anomalies in variable representation. Data organization and compatibility between variables were also reviewed.

Particular attention was given to municipality names, territorial identifiers, institutional classifications, and historical validity periods in order to preserve consistency between records and reduce potential analytical bias.

Overall, the evaluation suggested that the dataset maintains acceptable quality standards for exploratory analysis and future preprocessing after cleaning and standardization procedures are applied.

Statistical Summary

Descriptive Statistics

Statistical summaries were used to better understand the numerical behavior of digital government indicators and territorial measurements across municipalities and institutions. Descriptive statistics provide an initial understanding of variability, performance distribution, and numerical consistency.

Measures such as mean, minimum values, maximum values, variability, percentile behavior, and distribution patterns were considered to evaluate institutional performance indicators and historical digital development measurements.

Statistical exploration helps identify possible differences between municipalities and territorial entities, allowing comparisons between regions with different levels of digital maturity and institutional technological performance.

These summaries also support the identification of unusual values, possible anomalies, and variables that may require normalization, scaling, or additional preprocessing during future analytical stages.

Correlation Analysis

Variable Relationships

Correlation analysis was preliminarily considered to explore relationships between numerical variables and institutional performance indicators associated with digital government development across territorial entities.

The objective of this analysis is to identify whether numerical indicators exhibit similar behavior patterns, positive relationships, or possible dependencies that may contribute to understanding technological inequality and territorial digital maturity.

Variables related to performance scores, percentile measures, averages, and institutional indicators may present analytical relationships due to similarities in measurement structures and evaluation criteria.

Understanding these relationships may support feature selection, preprocessing decisions, dimensionality reduction, and future machine learning implementation by identifying the variables with the highest predictive potential.
